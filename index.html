Abstract

In this thesis, I wish to create a space for reflection on noise and language. Through myths as a lens, I look upon the landscape of Moldova as a place that informs identity; both an individual and a collective one. I start with myths and connect them to the Moldovan landscape. In this landscape, language and rituals serve as a political framework for the historical myths and geopolitics. I look upon those relations to understand the intentions of myths and how they relate to our times and collective memory. Can a dominant narrative change a place through myths? Whose narrative becomes the important one and which one remains suppressed? Returning to a period of colonization, I look into language as a tool for power and homogenization. If myths are a place, sound becomes the language of that place. When myths are used as a political tool to inform and suppress a collective identity, anything that does not fit into the narrative of the myths is considered noise. But can noise and listening serve as a space for resistance?

Introduction

What is a myth?


Myths have played a crucial role throughout human history in making sense of difficult natural concepts such as why it rains and why a landscape changes. By personifying phenomena or abstract concepts, myths provide a narrative framework that can make these concepts more accessible and relatable to human understanding. Myths are cultural common places. 1


Roland Barthes mentions that myths are a type of speech, as myths function as a system of communication that generates meaning. Myths are relatively simple stories that through the use of metaphors and other narration tools, cut to the core of what it means to be human. While new myths emerge from the most daily practices and everyday habits of contemporary life, ancient ones are continuously reinterpreted and contextualized within the present. Myth allows us to imagine another present, past and future, another way of being and living, and create bridges from the here and now to the there and then.2 As any stories, myths needs a place to live, a context to become alive. And as every piece of land has it’s own rules, what happens when they go out of the literature margins and become a political tool? Myths are both embedded in our everyday rituals, our language and our visual culture. When these become the result of historical political structures, is it still possible to reimagine another time free from such frameworks?


Mircea Eliade writes that a traditional society needs myths to live and to have a sense of belonging, meaning and security. The myth is regarded as a sacred story, and hence a "true history", because it always deals with the realities.3 Myths become a “role model” to follow as an “absolute truth”, in which we believe, when we detach from our personal values to act and follow such a “role model”. In this way, myths become a form of excellent cognitive thinking performed in the form of rituals.

Becoming

I grew up in Moldova. Moldova has been colonized many times, by the Roman Empire, the Ottoman Empire, the Russian Empire, and the Soviet Union. Thus we still carry the “noise” and traces from such histories and different colonizers in the form of food, rituals, celebrations, street names, and even speech on the land. In general terms, noise is considered unwanted or loud sounds, but in the context of colonization, I’m referring to noise as a symbol of the cultural and ecological pollution of a territory.

In the following lines, I’ll try to find answers to these questions by returning home. Not too far back in time; back to the last era of colonization, which effects I still hear today the most. I didn’t grow up directly in the Soviet Union, I experienced the effects of it through the subjective perspectives of my family, in the patterns that the country is still keeping. The story I will bring to you now is also a subjective one. I referring to the political ideology as a myth, the one that become role model for more then a decade and changed the collective identity and even memory, becouse its dominance.

Let’s begin with the invasion. In 1940 geo-political and geo-strategic interests from the Soviet Union, on the land of Moldova then named Bessarabia, became more intense. In order to hide their geo-strategic interests of occupying the territory, a more believable narrative was needed. According to their narrative, Moldovans is actually a separate nation with no link to Romania and closer in historical destiny and culture to the Russian, Ukrainian and other Soviet people’s history than to the Romanian nation.

So, the “becoming” of the Moldovan nation went through an aggressive intervention at many levels. From a political level the colonization meant to cancel a democratic institution and the instauration of a new ideology. As the main intention is to build a strong military and economic force, the soviet ideology meant a transformation and direction of a whole society. From a society level, it meant an instauration of new set of beliefs, that will facilitate the new idea/ ideology.

That mean the restructuring if the ideoA way of directing a whole society was through political myths. Ionuț Isac writes about political myths and define them as a subjective belief that provides a common foundational belief system of one’s origins, identities and goals on both an individual and collective national level: “Political myth can be said to fix its 'referent' at a confluence of objective and subjective, reality and fiction, which makes it no "weaker" than an elaborate social theory or ideology, but even more powerful than them when it comes to impressing and moving human communities and collectivities.” Political myths are not directly rooted in present reality, but instead they construct a future reality to make people act differently in a present, as if the constructed future already existed.

Excursion into a myth

Mythologies are
cultural common places

I invite you on a short walk through the capital of Moldova, Chisinau. What we have in front of you is the highest (15 meters!) monument in Chisinau of a hero, of a former country that already does not exist, Sergei Lazo. The monument is placed in front of a public park representing a mythological hero. Mythologies are cultural common places, recurrent narratives that are perceived as natural in a given culture but in fact were naturalized and their historical, political, or literary origins forgotten or disguised. Sergei Lazo is an example of such mythological common narrative.



The following lines are based on a documentary movie titled Lazo about Lazo as a mythological character from the Soviet Union, directed and written by Stefan Rusu.

When the Soviets colonized Moldova, a set of symbols was invented to prove the existence of Moldovan national identity, but also to rewrite and demystify the local history and its heroes. To recreate a collective memory of the local history and demystify it. The country had to deal with an updated version of the present and to reimagine the past; a form of past that served the soviet ideals. Sergei Lazo is a character and person who was reconstructed as a hero and who is an example of such recreation. He suddenly became a “hero” and perfectly fitted into soviet mythology. I’m referring to soviet mythology, to a soviet ideology that was promoted over the occupied land. In that recreation, he was suddenly dedicated to a series of monuments, street names, museums, squares, institutions and factories titles all around the country.

Lazo was born in Moldova into a noble family. During his study in Sankt Petersburg and Moscow, he was active in revolutionary circles. Later he joined the Bolshevik forces and participated in the Russian Civil War and had the position of the second front line. At the time, he was not very well known as a dominant figure or hero. In the context of the Civil War, when the Red Army excited his elite, the Soviets had to come up with a solution to supplement the lack of dominant figures. People from the frontline were used to create stories of heroes. So in the case of Moldova, the soviets made Lazo become a hero as part of a heroic war.


The legend says that Lazo was kept by the Japanese and burned alive in the oven of the Locomotive during the mission he had in Far East. No documentary information could be found about this legend, exept few artistic proofs, which nurtured and sustained the myth, but also gave a nationalistic feeling of the past.







One “proof” of the story was the novel for young adults written by a preeminent soviet novelist Aleksandeer Fadeev titled Kak pogib Sergeĭ Lazo, translated as The Death of Sergei Lazo. The novel is supported by a series of illustrations of the war and has Lazo as a dominant figure. He is described as being extremely courageous, good in math and with incredible oratorical capacities etc. A big part of the novel was dedicated to the death of Lazo; proofing the legend of being burned alive in a locomotive oven.

Alongside that, two film was made about Lazo, comsioned by Moldova Film, one was commissioning Andrei Tarkovsky together with Aleksander Gordon to make a future film about the national hero “Sergei Lazo”. Another one “The Life and Immortality of Sergei Lazo” (1985) by V. Pascaru. With an interesting end, where the voice over says “You need to find the truth even in the most uneven places… tragedy of personal life and death disappears when person feel himself as a unit of a whole humanity and recognizes the meaning of his existence in the result of his work and benefit that these abilities bring to…” The film made a substantial contribution to sustain the legend about Lazo

In the public space, monuments and streets named in the name of the new national hero were appearing. Like the one in park in Chisinau, named after him, the park Sergei Lazo. “ Brassier argues that a cognitive event is produced by noise that divides thought and feeling and has the potential to generate a new subject.” In Russia, nowadays, Lazo is not a hero anymore. The tragic death of Lazo and even his life have recently dismissed by researchers as “the country that made him a hero is no longer on the map” . Instead, the shadow, or maybe beter the symbol of the myth is still present in Chisinau.

On a decaying concrete platform, weakened through time, a base as a timeline reminds us that the myth is still with us, but we do not understand its face anymore, as its meaning does not mean anything anymore. A strong-standing hero morphing into a landscape, like a blind spot in our memory, is present, but absent. It is big, it is impressive and it creates a sense of an unknown anxiety - like a traumatic experience that goes through generations. How long can a myth keep living?

What we see

What we see

We didn’t move even one meter from Lazo Park, when I met you. Do you remember how we are in Chisinau now? Can you believe what you see in front of you? Mythologies are cultural common places, recurrent narratives that are perceived as natural in a given culture, but in fact were naturalized and their historical, political, or literary origins forgotten or disguised.

As part of creating a new collective identity, museums had to convey the myths as well. Right after the soviet occupation, all the existing collection works from the young established museum (opened in 1939) were loaded onto two wagons and shipped to Kharkiv in Ukraine. The existence of the works is still unknown today. The works in the museum were replaced by works donated by the Tretyakov State Gallery and A. S. Pushkin State Museum of Fine Arts in Moscow, as well as by the Leningrad State Hermitage.

To supplement the lost heritage, Moldovan Soviet Socialist Republic was funding museums to commission and purchase works of art - usually from local masters in the field of fine arts. Alongside, a series of donations were made by some collectors and prominent personalities in the field of arts from Bessarabia.

The system promoted realistic paintings, but was in opposition to the pseudo-realist style of socialist academism. A style that was advocated by the Academy of Arts of the USSR in the early post-war years. However, socialist academism was based on exaggerating to the point of absurdity with the forms of "fable", "content" and "narrative" in painting.

One of the works, acquired by the MSSR museum in the period of restoring the lost collection, was the painting “Glie și Oamenii” in translation “Lands and people”, by Moldovan painter Valentina Rusu-Ciobanu, exhibited at the exhibition “Glory to the work” in 1975. The work represent an image of the village during autumn, in a period of harvesting. The village is equipped with the most modern achievements of technological progress in the agro-industrial sphere, also showing the great improvements, the greate upgrade made by the state on a colonised land. Fused with happy looking characters of local communal work, dressed in perfect, clean clothes, running tractors, electricity towers, somenone is biking somewhere, running cars, a red car in front of the main village, as a sigh of communist party is duing its duties too, perfectly organised working life, actively implicated into a communal growth, a communal rhythm, a common place. Everyone is happy, no one human presence in the houses of the village, because everyone is on the land. The image that showing just a part of something big. What can go wrong?

This upgraded version of the Moldovan village, painted following the ideological criteria of those years, isn’t that simple and perfect. If we take a step back, the image dosen’t feel natural at all, even more, the portraited vilage do no exist. The painting is just an image of what the state wanted to see, a desired image of a village, a mythological image of the space. We are looking into an imagination, into a dream, into utopia, into a project that wants to change the humans and shape th land, in order to achieve its own goals. A “reality”, “virtual reality”, covered into a noise of dominant culture, where even is hard to depict were that “reality” is.

From another side, the painting is reflecting the spiritual part of the place, the transcendental. What dose it mean to be suppressed? The language of spiritual is hiden in the language of the myth, if we erase the ideological elements, if we take apart what “have to be”, then we might find a real village. The land is exploited, the culture is modified, the common place organised and re-organised, the myth limits, the language is a myth, metaphor.

Lands and People refers to the painting of Landscape with the Fall of Icarus by Pieter Bruegel, that can be interpreted as “the flight of Icarus symbolizes the flight of human thought and imagination; the human desire for new discoveries, achievements, conquests. In this case, the painting can be interpreted as the defeat of the sublime in the struggle against the commonplace, which does not care about those who strive for light".

Even thought the painting was made according to the ideological formula, the artist was accused by the party for being too abstract and formalistic, which meant the message was not too clear and it could produce additional meaning. Although the party did not even have a clear explanation on what abstract and formalistic meant, I guess it did not limit itself to the “reality” of the myth. The painting Lands and People, painted in the context of the myth is just an example of the tools used by the state. Could we relate to our time? Could our images, our technology to work in the same way?

Intertwine

Intertwine

Myths are considered a type of speech. Roland writes that language needs special conditions to become a myth. Relating this to the history of Moldova, the historical conditions that affected the language and its effects can still be heard till now.

To just remind you; we are still in front of the park in Chisinau. As you can hear, besides the sound of passing cars, you will hear an intertwining soundscape of voices mostly composed of Romanian and Russian. In our everyday life in common places, intertwined communication is something “natural”. It is something common. What kind of language do we speak? The answer to that is a long-lasting political and social dispute that goes back to 1991, when Moldova became independent. Moldovan or Romanian? And where is the difference?

Let me start by pointing to the fact that we are filling the gaps in the Romanian language with Russian words, even after declaration of independence, which count more then 30 years. But also after reforms, national movements of restoring the national language. In **** year, romanian language was contitutionaly recognised a national language. And even before that that was also a political tool, but in this case to use moldoval language to bring nostalgic feeling about colonial past.

I don’t want to sound nationalistic, when I write about the effects of colonization and I do acknowledge how no language is not affected by other languages anymore, but Romanian language carries traces of Russian colonization, which do not originate within the language. The noises that are blending the speech, the sentences, filling it till we can formulate a whole message are a trace of such history; forcing one language to translate itself into another. Till you can understand what we want to say. This is not accidental. These noises are political embedded codes in our unconsciousness.

During Soviet Union, language came to serve as a political tool, when it was used to strengthen the border, to “protect” the people and to isolate Moldova from Romania. It was a political tool when it came to “erase any national local differences and to transform the whole body of the country, to a brotherhood nation which speaks the same language”. One example is how the Latin alphabet script was changed to Cyrillic one. In order to expand the communist idology, and combat illiteracy. When the language of the colony did not have enough shapes for sounds, they had to invent a unique new letter to transcribe the Romanian phonetics. For example with g, ge, ghe /dʒ/, they made the sound zhe with breve “Ӂ ӂ”.The moving towards Cyrillics meant taking part in the national development and homogenizing the whole culture into a land of brothers and sisters. This homogenization meant excluding any minorities, as the whole culture was homogenized into national. Along with that transformation, another Russian language had apriveledged position, was a language of the state, formal language. As well russian was studied at schools, as well mixing russian and romanian (then moldovan language) school, in this way facilitating bilinvism.

The language went through a massive act of brushing from the Romanian words, along with literature, songs and anything that could remind or produce nostalgic feelings about the past. But overall, there is no difference between Romanian and Moldavian language. To see them as different is purely a political tool. The romanian words was excluded from literature, as it could recall the nostalgic feelings that the Soviet Union did not desire. Such feelings would mean uncertainty. In 1950 linguists from abroad started studying the Moldovan language and found that “the only difference they could conclude was writing with Cyrillic alphabet and minor mutation in diphthongs and the backing of some vowels after hard consonants. Tagkivini described it as a mere “dogma of soviet linguistics”. As such any differences are minor.

The national uprising and the de-russification processes came at the end of the 1980s, when Moldova reverted to the Latin spelling. In the text Land, water and Commas from 1987, Ion Druta refers to language as a limited source and as a natural resource, which was exploited like land. He writes that resources refer to culture, language, and history, which impacted the poverty of the spirit, the cosmos of man, the ecology of man and so the ecology of the soul. In addition to the damage caused by the phantasmagorical tendencies of the system, the language has also suffered a lot. Its charm has begun to disappear and instead turned into a dirty, grey language. A language that blocks even those who were participating in a conversation; no longer understanding what they want to say to each other.

In 2003 a controversial Romanian-Moldovan dictionary was published, being the first and only one of its kind. The book represented a sum of archaism, regionalism, and russificated words specific to the Moldovan area. The book pretends to show that the Moldovan language is something different from the Romanian one, but in fact is a sum of old and modified words, translated into modern romanian language, but from another side show the complexity of the language.

Even though I don’t know why, I still find it difficult to use words in my native language. There is a constant filter and restructuring of the meaning. Fear, anxiety, and hope to be understood. My language is intertwined between Romanian and Russian, and now even more complex, with English too. All those words come to fill the gaps. In the end, silence and errors are a common space; a metaphor for resistance.

Making noise

What are the characteristics of myths? Roland Barthes defines them as the transformation of a meaning into form. To give it a shape, as the meaning becomes a set of pre-designed signifiers. Within myths, meaning is never absent and any absence is considered noise. An example is informational entropy. It is a concept in physics and a metaphor in statistical analysis, which refers to the predictability of the message. A predictability as a calculation of information probability. The theory explains that information is structured in a set of possible answers that could cover all kinds of possible narratives. So the “freedom of choice” is actually a set of pre-designed choices. All possible errors are considered “noises”. The predictability of the constructed system helps to keep a control and understand the behavior of the system. In this way, myths become “naturalized”. Myths like to be in a shape that is steady and convincing. Everything outside such a form will be considered noise. But nothing is perfect and we can’t avoid errors and gaps. Just as grammar works as a framework for language of correctness, myths also carry a grammar. When something is not according to the grammar, it is wrong and limited to A or B. Uncertainty is what does not live up to the grammar. It is what’s considered noise. Noise becomes the refuse and a leftover generated by the process of information itself. Ray Brassier writes about noise in the article Against an Aesthetics of Noise. According to him, noise is more than an ambivalent object that relates to the true or false foundation. Insteads, it is a space where we can learn about our relation to information, as noise goes beyond the organized form of a system. In the book Listening Biennial, Daniela Medina Poch writes: “Listening allows a space for unlearning and relearning as listening requires being open towards what is being listened to.” As noise is what is refused by a dominant culture and everything outside myths therefore will be considered noise, to listen to such noise is a certain resistance, as noise allows a multitude of voices to speak together, when sound is an interconnected world and not a singular voice, as sound exists because of many relations within spaces and the relationship of cause and effect. As colonization is a voice that homogenizes by silencing the rest, decolonization instead should be focused on listening; not overshatter past voices, but to acknowledge those interconnections and a plurality of voices to co-exist. But to listen does not only remain within sound, it is also a concept that can be applied to history, when listening becomes a form of attention. To pay attention to what surrounds one, not overshatter with a singular worldview and to be open towards the voices and opinions of others. Within these pages, listening has served as a method of looking upon patterns within history and how they resulted in shaping a present Moldova, from which I am from. To look beyond a singular voice of the Soviet Union and instead listen to the relationships of different interconnections that ended up shaping both the Moldovan language, myths and landscape. To listen is to pay attention. And to listen to what has been framed as noise outside a dominant system is an act of resistance.
